Why Soiling Matters More Than You Think

If you installed solar to maximise savings, soiling losses are quietly eating your returns. A study of 500 rooftop systems across India by the National Institute of Solar Energy found that un-cleaned panels in Rajasthan lost an average of 28% of their generation capacity within six weeks of installation. In Mumbai and Chennai, the loss was 12–18% over the same period due to a combination of dust, soot, and pollution particulates.

The financial impact is direct. A 5 kW system generating ₹72,000 per year at full capacity loses ₹10,000–₹20,000 per year simply from accumulated dust — money that takes less than 20 minutes of cleaning each fortnight to recover.

"Dust is the silent thief of solar returns. It costs nothing to remove, yet most homeowners ignore it for months."

— Vijay Patil, Solar Echos Senior Engineer

Bird droppings deserve special attention. A single dropping on a solar cell creates a partial shading effect that, in series-wired strings, can disproportionately reduce the output of the entire string — not just the shaded cell. This is why droppings should be cleaned as soon as they are noticed, regardless of your regular schedule.

How Often to Clean by Region

Regional Cleaning Frequency Guide
  • Rajasthan / Gujarat: Every 10 days (dry season) · Every 3 weeks (post-monsoon)
  • Delhi NCR / UP: Every 14 days (winter smog season) · Every 3 weeks (summer)
  • Maharashtra (inland): Every 21 days year-round
  • Tamil Nadu / AP: Every 21 days (dry) · Monthly (post-monsoon — self-cleaning)
  • Coastal regions: Monthly DIY · Quarterly professional deionised water clean
  • Northeast / Kerala: Monthly (monsoon self-cleans panels effectively)

Step-by-Step Cleaning Method

  1. 1

    Clean in the early morning only

    Never clean panels in midday heat. The sudden temperature shock from cold water on hot glass can cause micro-cracks in the panel's encapsulant. Clean between 6–9 AM when panels are cool and dew has loosened surface dust.

  2. 2

    Use clean, preferably soft water

    Hard water leaves calcium deposits (white marks) that attract more dust and reduce transmittance. If your tap water is hard (>200 ppm TDS), use reverse osmosis (RO) reject water or filtered water. Rainwater is ideal.

  3. 3

    Apply water with a soft microfibre mop

    Use a telescoping handle mop with a microfibre head rated for solar panel cleaning. Wet the surface, then gently scrub with the damp mop in one direction (top to bottom). Do not use circular scrubbing motions that can trap grit.

  4. 4

    Rinse thoroughly from the top

    Use a gentle flow of water from a garden hose or watering can — never a pressure washer. Rinse top to bottom so dirty water drains away from already-cleaned sections.

  5. 5

    Allow to air dry — do not wipe

    Let the panels dry naturally in the morning air. Wiping with a dry cloth risks leaving fibres and micro-scratches on the glass surface. If water marks remain after drying, repeat with softer water next time.

5 Things You Must Never Use

Critical Warning

Using the wrong cleaning method can void your panel warranty and permanently reduce output. The five things below are the most common causes of warranty rejections in India.

  1. Detergent or soap: Leaves a residue film that increases dust adhesion. Even "rinsing off" rarely removes all detergent from the micro-textured panel surface. Use only clean water.
  2. Abrasive scrubbers, steel wool, or rough sponges: Scratch the anti-reflective coating on the glass, permanently reducing light transmission. Even a single scratch degrades that cell's output for the panel's lifetime.
  3. Pressure washers: The high-pressure jet can force water into the panel frame, junction boxes, and cable connectors, causing short circuits, corrosion, and voiding your IP65/IP68 rating.
  4. Alcohol or chemical cleaners: Degrade the EVA (ethylene vinyl acetate) encapsulant at the panel edges over time, eventually allowing moisture ingress — the leading cause of early panel failure.
  5. Cleaning during rain or immediately after: Wet panels carry live DC voltage. Working on a wet roof near live electrical connections is a serious electrocution risk. Always clean during dry conditions with the system isolated if possible.

Annual Professional Maintenance Checklist

Beyond regular cleaning, an annual professional inspection by a certified technician is essential. Here's what should be checked every 12 months.

Annual Service Checklist
  • Thermal imaging scan: Identifies hotspots, delamination, and cell cracks invisible to the naked eye
  • I-V curve trace: Measures panel output against factory spec to detect degradation beyond expected rate
  • DC wiring inspection: Check for rodent damage, UV degradation of cable insulation, loose MC4 connectors
  • Earthing continuity test: Verify that all panels, inverter, and mounting structure are properly earthed
  • Inverter log review: Check for error codes, grid fault records, and output vs expected generation
  • Mounting structure inspection: Check for corrosion, loose bolts, and roof penetration waterproofing integrity
  • Junction box inspection: Verify IP rating not compromised; check bypass diodes are functional

Pre-Monsoon Safety Checks

India's monsoon season brings its own hazards for solar systems. The following checks should be completed every year in April–May, before the monsoon arrives.

  1. Check all roof penetration seals. Mounting bolt holes, conduit entries, and earthing wire penetrations should be sealed with silicone-based waterproofing compound. Reseal any that show cracking or yellowing.
  2. Inspect earthing system. The monsoon brings lightning risk. Verify that your system's lightning arrester (if installed) and earthing rod are intact and the earthing resistance is below 10 ohms.
  3. Verify your inverter's IP rating. If the inverter is installed outdoors, ensure it is IP65 or higher. Water ingress during heavy rain is the most common cause of monsoon-season inverter failures.
  4. Enable surge protection. If your inverter supports it, check that DC and AC surge protection devices (SPDs) are active and not showing a fault indicator.
  5. Clear roof drainage. Blocked gutters and downpipes cause water to pool around panel mounting feet, accelerating metal corrosion. Clear all drainage paths before the first rains.
