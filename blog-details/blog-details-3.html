Why EV + Solar is a Powerful Combination

India is at the intersection of two energy revolutions simultaneously. Electric vehicle adoption is accelerating — with 1.7 million EVs sold in FY2025 — while rooftop solar installations crossed 15 GW for the first time. But most Indian homeowners are treating these as two separate decisions. They shouldn't.

When you combine a home solar system with an EV charger, the economics change dramatically. You are no longer just replacing grid electricity with solar-generated electricity — you are replacing petrol with sunlight. And that is a far more powerful financial transformation.

The Core Insight

A petrol car costs roughly ₹8 per kilometre in fuel alone. A grid-charged EV costs ₹2.50 per km. A solar-charged EV costs approximately ₹0.40–₹0.60 per km — and that cost stays flat for 25 years, while petrol prices keep rising.

How Solar EV Charging Works

The mechanics are simpler than most people expect. Your rooftop solar panels generate DC electricity, which your inverter converts to AC. This AC power feeds your home's electrical circuits. When your EV is plugged into its home charger, it draws from the same circuits — using solar-generated power first, then grid power if solar is insufficient.

A smart EV charger (like those compatible with the Tata Nexon EV or MG ZS EV) can be programmed to charge only when solar generation exceeds household consumption — meaning every unit stored in your car's battery came from the sun. Some advanced systems use a dedicated solar-to-EV DC fast charger that bypasses the home inverter entirely for even greater efficiency.

Sizing Your Solar System for an EV

Adding an EV charger meaningfully increases your household electricity consumption, which means your solar system needs to be larger than it would be for home use alone. The calculation is straightforward.

  1. 1

    Calculate your current home solar need

    Use your existing monthly consumption. A typical 3–4 BHK home consumes 300–500 units per month, requiring a 3–5 kW solar system.

  2. 2

    Estimate EV charging requirement

    Divide your monthly driving distance by your EV's efficiency (km per kWh). A Nexon EV does ~6.5 km/kWh. If you drive 1,500 km/month, you need ≈ 230 additional kWh per month.

  3. 3

    Add to your solar system requirement

    230 kWh/month ÷ (4.5 peak sun hours × 30 days) ≈ 1.7 kW additional solar needed. Round up to the nearest 1 kW panel increment.

  4. 4

    Verify roof space

    1 kW of solar requires approximately 100 sq ft of south-facing, shade-free roof area. Ensure you have the space before committing to a larger system.

Choosing the Right Home Charger

Not all home EV chargers are created equal when it comes to solar integration. Here's what to look for.

Charger Types for Solar Integration
  • Level 1 (3.3 kW AC): Standard 16A plug. Charges overnight. Works with solar but slow — best for scooters and city drivers.
  • Level 2 (7.4 kW AC): Dedicated 32A circuit. Ideal for most home solar integrations. Fully charges a Nexon EV in ~5.5 hours of peak solar.
  • Smart Charger with Solar Priority: Can communicate with your inverter to charge only from excess solar generation. Brands: Wallbox Pulsar Plus, ChargePoint Home, Delta AC Mini. Price: ₹25,000–₹45,000.
  • Bidirectional Charger (V2G/V2H): Allows energy to flow from your EV back to your home. Currently limited to specific models (Nissan Leaf, some upcoming Tata models). Price: ₹60,000–₹1,20,000.

Vehicle-to-Grid: Your Car as a Battery

The frontier of EV-solar integration is Vehicle-to-Grid (V2G) and Vehicle-to-Home (V2H) technology. Instead of buying a separate home battery storage system (which costs ₹80,000–₹2,50,000), you use your EV's battery — which you already own — as the storage unit.

During peak solar hours, the car charges from surplus solar. During the evening or a grid outage, it discharges back into the home, powering your lights, fans, and appliances overnight. A 40 kWh EV battery can power the average Indian home for 2–3 days without any grid connection.

Important Note

V2G in India is not yet commercially available at scale (as of July 2025). MNRE and BEE are drafting standards. It is expected to be available with select models by 2026–2027. If V2G compatibility is important to you, check the roadmap of your shortlisted EV brand before purchasing.

How to Get Started

  1. 1

    Plan together, not separately

    If you are buying an EV and planning solar, tell your solar installer upfront. They can size the system for both and include the EV charger circuit in the initial installation — saving you a second site visit and rewiring cost later.

  2. 2

    Apply for PM Surya Ghar with the combined system size

    You are eligible for subsidy on the full system, including the additional capacity for EV charging. A 6 kW system (3 kW home + 3 kW EV) qualifies for the full ₹78,000 central subsidy.

  3. 3

    Choose a smart charger with solar priority mode

    Invest in a smart charger that can read your inverter's excess generation signal. This ensures your car charges for free during peak solar hours rather than drawing grid power in the evening.

  4. 4

    Monitor and optimise

    Use the Solar Echos dashboard to track how much of your EV charging comes from solar vs grid. Most users achieve 70–85% solar charging with a correctly sized system.
