What is Net Metering?

Net metering is a billing arrangement between a solar homeowner and their electricity distribution company (DISCOM) that measures the difference between how much power you import from the grid and how much you export to it. When your solar panels generate more power than your home consumes — typically during peak daylight hours — the surplus flows into the grid. At night, or on cloudy days, you draw from the grid.

At the end of each billing cycle, you only pay for the net electricity consumed (import minus export). If you export more than you import, the surplus is carried forward as a monetary or unit credit. This mechanism is the single most important policy enabler of rooftop solar economics in India.

The Key Distinction

Net metering is not the same as selling electricity at market rates. Most Indian DISCOMs credit your export at the average power purchase cost (APPC) — typically ₹2.50–₹4.50 per unit — not at the retail tariff rate you pay to buy power. Some states offer higher rates as an incentive (see table below).

How Net Metering Billing Works

Your net meter records two values every billing cycle: units imported (grid → home) and units exported (solar → grid). The bill is calculated as follows.

Sample Bill Calculation (Maharashtra)
  • Units imported from grid this month: 320 units
  • Units exported to grid this month: 180 units
  • Net units consumed: 320 − 180 = 140 units
  • Bill at ₹8.50/unit (domestic slab): ₹1,190 (instead of ₹2,720 without solar)
  • Effective saving from exports: ₹1,530 this month

If your export exceeds your import in a given month, most states allow you to bank the surplus units and use them against future months' bills. The banking period and settlement terms vary by state — see the rules below.

How to Apply for Net Metering

  1. 1

    Complete your solar installation first

    Net metering cannot be applied for until your system is physically installed and your installer has submitted the commissioning application to the DISCOM on your behalf.

  2. 2

    Submit net metering application to DISCOM

    Your empanelled installer typically handles this. Documents required: installation certificate, single-line diagram, inverter specification sheet, your electricity consumer number, and Aadhaar-linked bank account details.

  3. 3

    DISCOM technical inspection

    A DISCOM engineer visits to verify the installation matches the approved specification and that the inverter anti-islanding protection meets grid code requirements. Timeline: 7–15 working days from application.

  4. 4

    Bidirectional meter installation

    Upon inspection approval, the DISCOM replaces your existing meter with a net meter (bidirectional meter) within 30 working days. The new meter records both import and export registers separately.

  5. 5

    Confirm your first net metering bill

    After meter installation, your next electricity bill should show both import and export readings. Verify that exports are being credited at the correct rate. If not, raise a billing complaint with your DISCOM immediately.

Gross Metering vs Net Metering

Some states and DISCOMs offer gross metering as an alternative, where 100% of your solar generation is exported to the grid at a fixed feed-in tariff, and you buy back all the power you consume at retail rates. This is generally only favourable when the feed-in tariff is higher than the retail tariff — which is rarely the case in India today.

For almost all residential and small commercial consumers in India, net metering is financially superior to gross metering, because the retail tariff you save by self-consuming (₹6–₹10/unit) is higher than the typical export credit rate (₹2.50–₹4.50/unit). Maximise self-consumption first; export the surplus.

Banking Rules — What Happens to Surplus Credits

When your exports exceed your imports in a given month, the surplus is "banked" — carried forward as a credit against future bills. Every state has its own rules on how long you can bank credits and what happens when the banking period expires.

Banking Rules Summary
  • Most states (including Gujarat, Maharashtra, Karnataka, UP): Credits banked for up to 12 months. Unused credits at year-end are paid out at the APPC rate or lapse (state-specific).
  • Tamil Nadu (TANGEDCO): Banking limited to 6 months. Surplus after 6 months lapses — this is a significant disadvantage that makes over-sizing less worthwhile in Tamil Nadu.
  • Delhi (BSES/TPDDL): Credits banking up to 12 months + an additional ₹2/unit generation incentive for 5 years. Best net metering package in India for small residential systems.
  • Practical tip: If your system regularly generates large surpluses, consider adding a battery storage system to self-consume more and reduce the risk of credits lapsing.

Your Rights if DISCOM Delays

The MNRE's Electricity (Rights of Consumers) Rules 2020 and subsequent guidelines give solar consumers legally enforceable timelines for net meter installation. If your DISCOM misses the mandated 30-working-day window, you have several recourse options.

Your Legal Rights

If the DISCOM fails to install your net meter within 30 working days of inspection approval, you can raise a grievance on the National Portal (pmsuryaghar.gov.in), file a complaint with the State Electricity Regulatory Commission (SERC), or approach the Electricity Ombudsman. Several SECRs have imposed penalties on DISCOMs for delays — and compensated homeowners retroactively from the inspection approval date.

Keep all correspondence with your DISCOM in writing (email or WhatsApp with read receipts). Document the dates of each interaction. If you escalate to the SERC, you will need to show a timeline of the DISCOM's delays — this documentation is your strongest tool.
